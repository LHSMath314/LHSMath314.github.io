High School Mathematics

These topics form the foundation of mathematical thinking — the essential tools used in nearly every higher branch.

Algebra

Equations, functions, and the language of mathematical relationships.

Geometry

Shapes, logic, and spatial reasoning — the oldest branch of math.

Trigonometry

Angles, triangles, and the circular functions that power physics.

Pre-Calculus

Preparing for limits, derivatives, and advanced functions.

Probability & Statistics

Understanding randomness, data, and real-world uncertainty.

AP Calculus

The math of change — motion, accumulation, and foundational analysis.

College-Level Mathematics

These fields expand your perspective, introducing new structures and powerful methods used in science, engineering, and pure math.

Calculus I–III

Deepening understanding of derivatives, integrals, vectors, and multivariate systems.

Linear Algebra

Matrices, vectors, transformations — the backbone of modern computation.

Differential Equations

Modeling systems that evolve over time, from physics to biology.

Discrete Mathematics

Logic, graphs, algorithms, and the foundation of computer science.

Proof Writing

Learning how to reason rigorously and communicate mathematical ideas.

Number Theory

The deep study of primes, patterns, and the fundamental nature of integers.

Complex Analysis

The world of complex functions — elegant, powerful, and full of surprises.

Beyond Mathematics

These topics venture into the frontier — areas where mathematics becomes abstract, creative, and sometimes mysterious.

Hypercomplex Numbers

Quaternions, octonions, and the strange algebras beyond the complex plane.

Real Analysis & Topology

The rigorous study of continuity, limits, shapes, and spaces.

Abstract Algebra

Groups, rings, and fields — the structures underlying modern math.

Differential Geometry

The mathematics of curves, surfaces, and the shape of space itself.

Tensor Calculus

The language of relativity, spacetime curvature, and modern physics.

Mathematical Logic

Foundations of truth, computability, and the limits of knowledge.

Frontier Structures

Undiscovered algebras and theoretical systems still waiting for purpose.

How They All Connect

Mathematics is not a collection of isolated subjects — it is a single, interconnected universe. Algebra leads to vector spaces used in linear algebra. Trigonometry and calculus become tools for differential equations. These ideas merge into multivariable calculus, which becomes the language of physics. Abstract algebra and topology describe symmetry and space. And beyond all of them lie deeper structures waiting to be uncovered.

As you explore these branches, you’re not just learning topics — you’re discovering the patterns that shape the world itself.
